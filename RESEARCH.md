
Visual Intuition for Acids and Bases
Why this project is worth building

This project is tightly aligned with the current AP Chemistry framework. Unit 8 carries an exam weighting of 11–15% on the multiple-choice section, and the framework spans pH/pOH, strong and weak acids/bases, acid-base reactions, titrations, molecular structure, pH versus pK_a, buffers, the Henderson–Hasselbalch equation, buffer capacity, and pH-sensitive solubility. In other words, this is not a narrow side-topic; it is one of the densest conceptual units in the course.

The current AP framework from College Board also points in exactly the direction your idea is taking. Its sample instructional activities for Unit 8 explicitly recommend having students make particulate-level drawings to explain buffering ability, and it specifically suggests using a virtual lab to create a buffer with a target pH after strong acid is added. That is close to your core premise: formulas should be explained by visible chemical change, not treated as detached algebra.

The framework also gives a useful scope warning. It says that AP does not assess derivation of the Henderson–Hasselbalch equation, does not assess quantitative pH change after acid/base is added to a buffer, and does not assess quantitative calculations of solubility as a function of pH. That means the best version of this project is not a “solve every textbook variant” engine. It is a conceptual visualization tool that makes the legal next step obvious and shows why it is legal.
What the strongest prior art already does

The field you are entering already has real prior art, but it is fragmented. In the tools and papers I found, the existing work falls into four families: exploratory simulations, virtual-lab/problem systems, gameified review environments, and specialized protonation-state visualizers. What is still missing is a tool that cleanly fuses all four: AP-aligned worked problems, particle-level animations, guided step ordering, and a game-like progression in one web-native experience.

The strongest open exploratory simulations come from PhET Interactive Simulations. pH Scale: Basics lets students test everyday liquids such as coffee, spit, and soap and observe how adding more liquid or diluting with water affects pH. pH Scale expands that idea into common-solution, micro, and custom-solution screens. Acid-Base Solutions asks learners to compare strong and weak acids and bases using pH paper, probes, and conductivity tools, and to investigate how concentration and strength affect pH. These are excellent for free exploration and intuitive cause-and-effect. Their weakness, for your purposes, is that they are mostly sandbox experiences, not AP-style guided reasoning sequences.

The most AP-aligned virtual-lab ecosystems come from ChemCollective, the American Association of Chemistry Teachers, and the Royal Society of Chemistry. ChemCollective offers unknown acid/base titration, buffer-design tasks, scaffolded “How Buffers Work” tutorials, walkthroughs for predicting buffer pH, and activities on buffer capacity; its virtual lab is explicitly designed to link chemical computations with authentic lab chemistry. AACT’s current AP acid-base titration simulation is especially relevant: it gives randomized titration curves, a graph trace tool, equivalence and half-equivalence reasoning, K_a/K_b calculation, hints, answer checking, and resets for repeated practice. RSC’s titration screen experiment adds context-rich levels and gamified points, including an acid-contaminated river, aspirin analysis, and ammonia analysis. These tools are strongest when students already know the mechanics and need practice reading curves and executing calculations. Their weakness is that the molecular causality behind each step is still mostly implicit.

There is also meaningful gameified prior art. The 2025 paper Questing through Acid–Base Chemistry describes an acid-base RPG with levels, NPC interactions, quiz elements, simulations, virtual experiments, scoring, feedback, animations, and reported positive feasibility/practicality results. Acid Base Global uses the escape-room format to review acid-base reactions, pH, neutralization, ampholytes, and Brønsted–Lowry theory, and its first classroom implementation found that students viewed the format as challenging but fun. A broader chemistry-game article, Chemistry and Chaos, argues that full chemistry RPGs remain comparatively uncommon and notes that chemistry role-play has more often appeared in choose-your-own-adventure-style lab activities than in fully integrated role-playing systems. Outside digital spaces, there are also card and board games such as Acid–Base Poker and hybrid games for reviewing organic acids and bases, but these are usually review-centered rather than explanation-centered.

A more specialized and very relevant piece of prior art is the pH ruler applet. It was built to help students visualize protonation states as pH changes, allowing them to drag a slider and watch the abundance of different protonation states change in real time. That is conceptually powerful, especially for pH versus pK_a and protonation/deprotonation intuition. Its weakness is scope: it is a narrow visualizer, not a general AP Chemistry Unit 8 environment.
What the research says students actually need

The chemistry-education research strongly supports your instinct that acid-base formulas need visual grounding. One long-running idea in the literature is Johnstone’s triplet: chemistry understanding requires students to coordinate the macroscopic, submicroscopic, and symbolic levels. RSC practitioner/research summaries note that students struggle to move between these representations, and recent Journal of Chemical Education work reports that computer simulations can improve students’ ability to translate among chemical representations. Other work reports that dynamic particulate-level simulations help students understand particle-based chemistry more effectively. A broader meta-analysis by SRI found that simulations have a beneficial effect over non-simulation instruction, and that simulations with enhancements such as dynamic representations or added support outperform plain simulations.

Acid-base topics produce a particularly dangerous kind of “math-first” understanding. Research on student conceptions of buffers found that students often rely heavily on the Henderson–Hasselbalch equation while lacking the conceptual resources to use it productively, especially resources connected to proton donation/acceptance and pH relative to pK_a. A related dissertation project found that when instruction becomes too calculation-heavy, students can shift into a purely mathematical epistemology instead of chemical sensemaking; the same work reported that graphical visualization activities improved students’ activation of more productive resources for buffer reasoning.

Several specific misconceptions recur across the literature. Students confuse strong vs. weak with concentrated vs. dilute; they know pH labels but often do not understand the logarithmic meaning of the pH scale; and they routinely struggle with titration reasoning at equivalence and half-equivalence, including what species dominate at each stage and why pH at half-equivalence reveals pK_a. Research on acid-base reaction reasoning also shows that many students cannot clearly describe what is happening on the molecular level during proton transfer. These are not small edge cases. They are exactly the places where a stepwise, visual, guided activity has the highest payoff.

The instructional implication is straightforward: your project should not merely display equations. It should make each region of a problem visibly different. Students should be able to see when stoichiometric consumption is the dominant story, when equilibrium becomes the dominant story, when a conjugate pair has emerged, when a ratio is governing pH, and when a graph point is meaningful rather than arbitrary. That is where the present prior art is still thin, and where your project can be strongest.
The game design that has the highest payoff

The clearest design opportunity is to build the missing connective tissue between the best existing categories. PhET is excellent at intuitive manipulation, ChemCollective and AACT are excellent at AP-style practice, RSC is good at contextualized challenge structure, and the acid-base RPG / escape-room literature is good at motivation and progression. The most distinctive version of your project is therefore not “yet another simulation” and not “yet another quiz.” It is a guided visual reasoning engine that lets the student decide what to do next, then makes the consequences visible at the particulate, symbolic, and graphical levels all at once.

The strongest interaction loop is: observe → predict the next legal step → commit → calculate → animate the state change → explain the new state. That loop fits the inquiry emphasis in simulation research, and it directly counters the documented habit of plugging into Henderson–Hasselbalch or an ICE setup before students understand what species are actually present. A useful rule for every screen is that the learner should always answer two questions before being allowed to compute: “What is in excess?” and “What pair controls the chemistry now?”

The interface should keep multiple representations visible by default. A high-value layout would be a beaker/species panel, a calculation/decision panel, and a graph/number-line panel. When a weak acid is titrated with strong base, for example, the graph should update, the particle view should show HA decreasing and A⁻ increasing, and the explanation panel should explicitly unlock the logic for why Henderson–Hasselbalch becomes appropriate after stoichiometric neutralization has created a conjugate pair. A global pH–pK_a ruler inspired by the pH ruler applet would be especially useful because it can reappear across buffer, indicator, and protonation-state screens.

Wrong answers should be misconception-driven rather than random. Good distractors include: treating strong as concentrated, using Henderson–Hasselbalch before stoichiometry, assuming equivalence is always pH 7, choosing an indicator far from the equivalence-point pH, or forgetting that weak-acid solutions remain mostly un-ionized. AACT’s use of hints, tolerances, answer checking, and repeated randomized resets is a very strong model here: feedback should nudge the student back toward the underlying chemical story, not just mark an answer wrong.

The narrative layer should be light. The prior art suggests that story, context, levels, and points help with engagement, but a full RPG is not the highest-return use of your time in a solo build. A better fit for your schedule is a “quest board” built from authentic AP-style contexts: a contaminated stream, aspirin analysis, household buffering, antacid troubleshooting, pool chemistry, or unknown-acid identification. That gives you enough motivation and coherence to feel game-like without forcing you to build a dialogue-heavy world.
The minimum viable problem set that best fits your deadline

For a solo build in the April-to-May window, the most defensible scope is four deep modules plus one stretch module. That is enough to show technical originality and curricular breadth without spreading the project thin.

    Strong versus weak at equal molarity. This should show the same nominal concentration producing different particle distributions, conductivity, and pH, then tie that behavior back to conjugate-base stabilization and structure. It covers strong/weak reasoning and molecular structure while directly targeting one of the most persistent misconceptions in acid-base chemistry.

    Neutralization that becomes a buffer. Start with weak acid + strong base or weak base + strong acid, force a stoichiometric bookkeeping step first, then animate the appearance of the conjugate pair and unlock buffer reasoning only after that transition. This is the single best “vertical slice” because it touches Topics 8.4, 8.8, and 8.9 and mirrors the exact kind of simulation-based task the AP framework recommends.

    Weak-acid titration explorer. Let students scrub along the pH curve, watch species ratios shift, locate equivalence and half-equivalence, infer pK_a at half-equivalence, and choose a suitable indicator. This aligns with the AP titration topics and borrows the strongest graph-based mechanics from AACT and RSC without inheriting their mostly symbolic presentation.

    Buffer capacity stress test. Keep the buffer ratio constant while changing total concentration, then separately alter the acid/base ratio and compare what changes. This is an unusually high-value visual module because students can watch pH stay similar while buffer capacity changes, or watch capacity become asymmetric when one component dominates. It covers Topic 8.10 better than most current tools do.

    Stretch module on pH and solubility. Make this qualitative, not algebra-heavy. A slider that changes pH while a sparingly soluble salt dissolves or precipitates is enough, because AP expects qualitative Le Châtelier reasoning here rather than full pH–solubility calculations. This is a great polish module if the first four are stable.

If there is time after those five pieces, the next best extension is a qualitative polyprotic-acid titration screen. The framework includes polyprotic titration reasoning, but it explicitly excludes full species-computation demands, which makes a visual “multiple peaks / multiple deprotonations” treatment a good stretch goal rather than an MVP requirement.
The build plan most likely to succeed by May

Because the project is a solo effort, the technical architecture should be scenario-driven, not screen-by-screen handcrafted. The most efficient design is one reasoning/animation engine fed by scenario data: initial species, volumes, concentration values, allowed next steps, graph parameters, misconception distractors, hints, and animation states. That recommendation follows directly from the strongest prior art systems, which succeed by reusing frameworks across many resettable scenarios rather than by building each one from scratch. ChemCollective does this through families of virtual-lab/tutorial activities, and AACT does it through repeated randomized titration runs with the same core interaction pattern.

The best first vertical slice is the neutralization-becomes-a-buffer module. It is the most strategic starting point because it naturally forces the transition from stoichiometry to equilibrium to pH reasoning, which is exactly where many students break down. A successful version of that one module will already prove the value of the whole project. If it works by May 3, the rest of the schedule becomes much more realistic: May 6 can be spent cloning the engine across strong-vs-weak and titration variants instead of inventing new interaction patterns from scratch.

The user-study phase should be short but genuine. PhET’s own design process emphasizes iterative cycles that can include student interviews, redesign, and classroom usage, which is a strong reminder that learnability is not automatic even when the chemistry is correct. For your project, the best test is not “Did they enjoy it?” first, but “Did they choose the correct next step, and could they explain why?” Good pre/post prompts would target the exact known pain points: strong vs. weak, logarithmic pH meaning, what happens at half-equivalence, and when Henderson–Hasselbalch is legitimate.

The strongest final version of this project, then, is not a full fantasy RPG and not a generic simulation sandbox. It is a polished AP Chemistry reasoning experience built around a small number of excellent cases, each one asking students to decide the next step, execute it, and see the chemistry unfold. That would place the project in a strong position relative to the existing field: the best current prior art separates exploration, practice, and play, while your concept can unify them around the one thing students most often lack in Unit 8—visual chemical intuition.
